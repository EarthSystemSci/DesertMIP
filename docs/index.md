# DesertMIP (Desertification Model Intercomparison Project)
**A MIP registered with the WCRP-CMIP7 framework**

---

## About DesertMIP
Deserts are the primary natural source of atmospheric mineral dust, accounting for ~75% of global aerosols, and exhibit extreme climate sensitivity ("desert amplification"). However, current Earth System Models (ESMs) within the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project (CMIP) framework typically rely on static desert masks. 

This critical limitation induces systematic biases in dust emission simulations (up to 100%) and regional warming projections. **DesertMIP** is designed to address this gap by providing the first multi-model assessment of *dynamic* desert boundaries and their subsequent dust-climate feedbacks in CMIP7-class models.

---

## Core Scientific Objectives
By introducing satellite-derived dynamic desert fractions as a novel forcing, DesertMIP focuses on three primary pillars:

1. **Global Dynamics & Accurate Boundary Mapping:** Systematically determining and tracking the dynamic shifts in global desert boundaries using high-resolution observational data (e.g., ESA WorldCover 2020).
2. **Automated Predictive Modeling:** Developing a robust framework to automatically project future desert boundaries and spatial transitions based on specific climate change trajectories and probabilities (utilizing scenarios such as SSP2-4.5).
3. **Impacts & Climate Hazards:** Evaluating the cascading impacts of these shifting boundaries on regional climate, atmospheric circulation, and associated climate hazards (e.g., severe dust storms, extreme heat events, and altered precipitation patterns).

---

## Cross-MIP Synergies
DesertMIP strongly aligns with the broader CMIP7 objectives and seeks synergistic collaborations to maximize data utility and reduce redundant simulations. We actively coordinate with:
* **LMIP (Land Surface, Snow and Soil Moisture MIP):** Exploring intersections in dynamic land-use/land-cover changes and land surface processes.
* **AerChemMIP (Aerosols and Chemistry MIP):** Investigating the subsequent effects of emitted mineral dust on atmospheric aerosols, chemistry, and radiative forcing.
